Hispanic Youth in Washington D.C. Combat Stigmas and Negative Perceptions of their Community Through Art

Daniella Ortega, LAYC Mentor

Instead of going home afterschool to play video games, 10 students are spending their time working together and creating art in the program “Mi Vida, Mi Voz.” For those of you who don’t speak Spanish, this translates to “My Life, MY Story.” In this program, students are working together to combine art, photograph, and painting/drawing to create a story that reflects their lives and experiences growing up in the Hispanic or Latino community. This program also pairs groups with students from the University of Maryland, such as myself. I came across the opportunity to become a mentor for the program through flyer in the art department. I decided to apply because I wanted to become more active in my community through art – and I wanted an internship for my resume too. After being accepted and trained as a mentor, and having go through three weeks of the program, I have seen how rewarding this job as been. I could not have imagined the impact I have been able to make through helping the students create their project, and find their voice through art. Currently, we are working to get ready for the upcoming final exhibit, where the art of the students will be showcased. I am excited for the event, and I hope that there is a good turn out because everyone should see the work that these amazing kids have created. As much as I have taught the students about art, they have taught me lessons of being courageous and prideful of my culture. I am excited to come back again next fall semester, and I hope other college students will join.
